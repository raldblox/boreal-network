## Core Selection Rule

Boreal should serve users who already feel pain between asking for work and actually getting it completed.

The strongest early users usually have all or most of these traits:

- high-importance work
- fuzzy or incomplete intake
- outside help required
- mixed human and AI execution likely
- need for proof, review, or payout clarity
- willingness to pay for the outcome

## ICP Matrix

## 1. Primary Users

### A. Founder-led startups buying urgent external digital work

Typical roles:
- founder
- cofounder
- founding operator
- technical founder

Typical asks:
- AI automation implementation
- workflow rescue and cleanup
- MVP or prototype execution
- research or synthesis that must become action
- growth systems that need setup and follow-through

Why they fit:
- speed matters more than process theater
- scope is often fuzzy at intake
- they already buy outside help
- they care about proof and closeout
- they are willing to try a concierge-heavy first version if it gets real work done

What they expect:
- fast clarification
- clear ownership
- visible progress
- proof that the job is actually done
- less tool switching and less chasing

Why Boreal can win:
- better intake than listing-first marketplaces
- better continuity than chat and email
- better proof and closeout than generic PM tools

### B. SMB operators coordinating external specialists for messy digital work

Typical roles:
- operations lead
- head of growth
- product lead
- technical lead
- owner-operator

Typical asks:
- AI-assisted service implementation
- ops workflow repair
- research plus execution
- specialist work that needs review and acceptance

Why they fit:
- they live in the gap between demand and completion
- they often need flexible external capacity
- they care about accountability more than browsing supply catalogs

What they expect:
- one place to track what is happening
- clean handoffs between contributors
- proof and approval structure
- repeatable use for the next request

## 2. Secondary Users

### A. Curated specialist suppliers and leads

Typical roles:
- automation builder
- technical operator
- implementation specialist
- researcher
- creative or media operator
- lead fulfiller coordinating sub-work

Why they matter:
- early Boreal success depends on strong supply quality
- great suppliers create the proof loop that makes repeat demand possible

What they expect:
- clearer briefs
- less vague inbound work
- explicit proof requirements
- cleaner payout confidence
- reputational credit from completed work

Why secondary, not primary:
- demand quality comes first
- supply experience should be designed around the buyer wedge, not around broad lead generation

### B. Small teams already using freelancers plus AI tools, but with poor continuity

Typical pain:
- requests scattered across Slack, email, docs, and contractor chats
- repeated restatement of context
- no strong closeout trail
- weak ownership after the first reply

Why they matter:
- they feel the fragmentation problem sharply
- they can become strong repeat accounts

Why secondary:
- they are often adjacent to the main wedge, not cleaner than it

### C. Agent and runtime operators working on bounded, review-heavy requests

Typical roles:
- agent owner
- internal operator using local runtime
- workflow engineer managing mixed automation plus human review

Why they matter:
- they fit Boreal's deeper product thesis
- they help validate the human-plus-AI execution layer

Why secondary:
- if marketed too early, they can pull Boreal into infrastructure language instead of buyer language

## 3. Expansion Users

### A. Private supply networks and enterprise operator lanes

Why attractive later:
- stronger repeat demand
- better control and trust
- larger budgets
- more durable workflow standardization

Why not first:
- heavier expectations around compliance, controls, and integrations
- slower sales motion
- higher implementation burden

### B. Team and swarm fulfillment for more complex request classes

Why attractive later:
- compounds Boreal's network logic
- creates stronger defensibility around routing and coordination

Why not first:
- requires strong proof systems and role clarity first
- easier to overbuild before core request flow is proven

## 4. Explicitly Excluded Early Users

### A. Cheapest-labor buyers

Why exclude:
- they compress margin
- they reward volume, not quality
- they do not care enough about proof and continuity

### B. Casual freelancers seeking generic lead flow

Why exclude:
- this pulls Boreal toward a commodity marketplace
- Boreal's early edge is completion quality, not listing volume

### C. Teams with fully scoped, fully automatable, low-ambiguity workflows

Why exclude:
- they may be better served by internal workflow software or standard SaaS tools
- Boreal is strongest where ambiguity and mixed execution still matter

### D. Large enterprises expecting full compliance and polished transformation software on day one

Why exclude:
- long cycles
- heavy trust burden
- high product completeness expectations too early

### E. Hobbyists who mainly want a better chatbot

Why exclude:
- weak willingness to pay
- weak alignment with proof, payout, and closeout
- high risk of distraction from the core commercial wedge

## Primary Buyer Summary

The first buyer should usually be:

- a founder
- an operator
- a product or technical lead
- an SMB owner with urgent execution pressure

The first buyer is not buying architecture.
The first buyer is buying a better path from messy ask to completed result.

## Top Request Classes To Prioritize

1. AI implementation and automation work
2. workflow rescue and cleanup
3. MVP, specification, and prototype execution
4. research plus action-oriented output
5. specialist digital work that needs review, proof, and closeout

## Screening Questions For Early Demand

A strong early Boreal lead usually answers yes to several of these:

- Is this request important enough to pay for now?
- Is the ask still somewhat fuzzy or evolving?
- Will AI likely help, but not fully finish the job alone?
- Will the buyer want proof, review, or acceptance before closing?
- Will multiple steps or contributors likely be involved?
- Is the buyer frustrated by current tool fragmentation?

If mostly no, the lead may be a weak fit for the first wedge.

## Positioning Implication

Boreal should be packaged first for:

high-importance external digital work requests where the buyer needs continuity, proof, and accountable mixed human-plus-AI execution.

That is much stronger than trying to sound universal too early.
